# Dimension 4: ENAQT Beyond Photosynthesis -- Other Biological and Synthetic Systems

## Research Summary

This report investigates whether Environment-Assisted Quantum Transport (ENAQT) has been demonstrated or proposed in systems beyond photosynthetic complexes. The key finding is that **while ENAQT has been extensively studied in photosynthesis, there is growing evidence and theoretical proposals for ENAQT-like phenomena in several non-photosynthetic systems, most notably microtubule tryptophan networks, ion channel selectivity filters, organic molecules, and various synthetic platforms. However, direct experimental demonstrations of ENAQT outside photosynthesis remain limited.**

## 1. Microtubules and Cytoskeletal Proteins

### 1.1 Feasibility of Coherent Energy Transfer in Microtubules (Craddock et al., 2014)

```
Claim: Computational analysis demonstrates that the unique tryptophan (Trp) network within individual tubulin dimers can possess significant dipolar couplings capable of supporting quantum coherent beating effects similar to those observed in the FMO photosynthetic complex, with coherent beatings lasting ~600 fs at physiological temperature.
Source: Journal of the Royal Society Interface
URL: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4191094/
Date: 2014
Excerpt: "Population dynamics calculations from the Haken & Strobl method were applied to the Hamiltonian... It was determined that coherent beatings last for approximately 600 fs with a pure dephasing rate of 50 cm-1... We conclude that, based on this initial analysis, the unique Trp network within an individual tubulin dimer can possess significant dipolar couplings capable of supporting quantum coherent beating effects similar to those observed in the FMO photosynthetic complex."
Context: This was a purely computational study using the Haken-Strobl method applied to tubulin's tryptophan network. The authors explicitly state their goal is to stimulate future experimental work.
Confidence: Medium (computational prediction, not experimentally verified)
```

### 1.2 Electronic Energy Migration in Microtubules -- Experimental (Kalra et al., 2023)

```
Claim: Experimental measurements using tryptophan autofluorescence lifetimes demonstrate that electronic energy can diffuse over 6.6 nm in microtubules, comparable to some photosynthetic complexes. Energy transport as explained by conventional F"orster theory does not sufficiently explain observations, and anesthetics reduce exciton diffusion.
Source: ACS Central Science
URL: https://pubs.acs.org/doi/10.1021/acscentsci.2c01114
Date: 2023
Excerpt: "Here, we use tryptophan autofluorescence lifetimes to probe energy hopping between aromatic residues in tubulin and microtubules. By studying how the quencher concentration alters tryptophan autofluorescence lifetimes, we demonstrate that electronic energy can diffuse over 6.6 nm in microtubules... Energy transport as explained by conventional F"orster theory (accommodating for interactions between tryptophan and tyrosine residues) does not sufficiently explain our observations. Our studies indicate that microtubules are, unexpectedly, effective light harvesters."
Context: This is the key experimental paper demonstrating long-range energy migration in microtubules. The 6.6 nm diffusion length is on the order of a single tubulin dimer volume. Anesthetics (etomidate and isoflurane) were shown to reduce exciton diffusion.
Confidence: High (peer-reviewed experimental study in ACS Central Science)
```

### 1.3 Quantum Information Flow in Microtubule Tryptophan Networks (Gassab et al., 2026)

```
Claim: A Lindblad master equation analysis of tryptophan networks in microtubules reveals that superradiant components drive rapid export of correlations to the environment, while subradiant components retain them. Static energetic and structural disorder suppress long-range transport, but ordered lattices sustain long-range correlated coherence.
Source: arXiv / PMC
URL: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12938935/
Date: 2026 (preprint 2024)
Excerpt: "Superradiant components drive the rapid export of correlations to the environment, whereas subradiant components retain them and slow their leakage. Embedding single tubulin units into larger dimers and spirals reshapes pairwise correlation maps and enables site-selective routing. Scaling to larger ordered lattices strengthens both export and retention channels, whereas static energetic and structural disorder suppress long-range transport and reduces overall correlation transfer."
Context: This paper provides a quantum information-theoretic framework for understanding energy/information flow in microtubule tryptophan networks. It identifies conditions for preserving nonclassical correlations.
Confidence: Medium (preprint/early publication)
```

### 1.4 Quantum Relaxation in Microtubule Tryptophan System (Shirmovsky & Shulga, 2023)

```
Claim: Investigation of quantum relaxation in microtubule tryptophan chains shows that decoherence time depends on temperature and can be significantly increased by decreasing temperature. The tunneling effect and lattice vibrations impact quantum relaxation processes.
Source: Physica A: Statistical Mechanics and its Applications
URL: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S037843712300242X
Date: 2023
Excerpt: "The paper investigates the mechanism of quantum relaxation in a microtubule tryptophan chain on the basis of the Born-Markov formalism... The obtained results demonstrate an obvious dependence of the relaxation on the probability of tunneling through the potential."
Context: This Russian group has published extensively on quantum effects in microtubules, including entangled state transfer protocols and quantum relaxation effects. Their work suggests that decoherence times can be engineered by temperature control.
Confidence: Medium (theoretical work from a specialized group)
```

### 1.5 Oxidative Species-Induced Excitonic Transport in Tubulin (Kurian et al., 2017)

```
Claim: Reactive oxygen species (ROS) can induce excitonic transport in tubulin aromatic networks, with potential implications for neurodegenerative disease. The tryptophan mega-networks in microtubules (up to 10,000 residues) support quantum states through collective and cooperative effects.
Source: Journal of Photochemistry and Photobiology B: Biology
URL: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1011134417303066
Date: 2017
Excerpt: From related coverage: "Trp mega-networks support quantum states in proteins through collective and cooperative effects. When multiple Trp chromophores are organized in a network, their transition dipoles can interact with each other, leading to the emergence of superradiant and subradiant eigenmodes."
Context: This work connects microtubule quantum effects to ROS biology and neurodegeneration, suggesting that oxidative stress may modulate quantum transport in cytoskeletal networks.
Confidence: Medium (speculative connection to disease)
```

## 2. Ion Channels

### 2.1 Noise-Assisted EET in Ion Channel Selectivity Filter (Bassereh et al., 2015)

```
Claim: A toy model of the ion channel selectivity filter backbone demonstrates that introducing dephasing in disordered chains leads to weakening of localization effects and increases the efficiency of quantum energy transfer -- a direct ENAQT mechanism. The presence of noise is crucial for enhancement of EET efficiency in disordered chains.
Source: Journal of Physics: Condensed Matter
URL: https://arxiv.org/pdf/1408.6256
Date: 2015
Excerpt: "In fact, we show that such expectation is valid for ordered chains. However, our results indicate that introducing the dephasing in the disordered chains leads to the weakening of the localization effects, arising from the multiple back-scatterings due to the randomness, and then increases the efficiency of quantum energy transfer. Thus, the presence of noise is crucial for the enhancement of EET efficiency in disordered chains."
Context: This is one of the most direct ENAQT proposals outside photosynthesis. The selectivity filter of ion channels (e.g., KcsA) has a backbone of carbonyl groups that may exhibit quantum coherence, and noise assists energy transfer in this disordered biological structure.
Confidence: High (peer-reviewed, direct ENAQT demonstration in a non-photosynthetic biological system)
```

### 2.2 Excitation Energy Transport with Noise and Disorder in Selectivity Filter (Jalalinejad et al., 2018)

```
Claim: Disorder in the ion channel selectivity filter backbone considerably reduces excitation energy transfer efficiency, but the addition of noise helps recover high EET for a wide range of system parameters -- confirming the ENAQT mechanism.
Source: Journal of Physics: Condensed Matter
URL: https://iopscience.iop.org/article/10.1088/1361-648X/aadeab
Date: 2018
Excerpt: "Our results show that disorder in the backbone considerably reduces EET, but the addition of noise helps to recover high EET for a wide range of system parameters. Our analysis may help for better understanding of the coordination of ions in the filter as well as the fast and efficient functioning of the selectivity filters in ion channels."
Context: Extends the Bassereh et al. 2015 work with more detailed modeling of the selectivity filter environment. The high throughput (10^8 ions/sec) and selectivity (1:10^4) of ion channels may be partially enabled by ENAQT.
Confidence: High (peer-reviewed computational study)
```

### 2.3 Quantum Coherence in Ion Channels: Resonances, Transport and Verification (Vaziri & Plenio, 2010)

```
Claim: Quantum coherence in ion channels can play a role in mediating ion-conduction and ion-selectivity. Vibrational excitons in ionophores can serve as experimental probes for quantum coherence-assisted ion transport.
Source: New Journal of Physics
URL: https://iopscience.iop.org/article/10.1088/1367-2630/12/8/085001/pdf
Date: 2010
Excerpt: "A common feature in a large number of ion channels is the presence of a gate that can be activated by chemicals, voltage, light or mechanical... Quantum coherence in ion channels: resonances, transport and verification."
Context: This influential paper by Vaziri and Plenio laid groundwork for considering quantum coherence effects in ion channels, which has been built upon by Salari, Bernroider, and others.
Confidence: Medium (theoretical proposal)
```

### 2.4 Quantum Thermodynamics and Coherence in Ion Channels (Bhattacharya & Roy, 2013)

```
Claim: Quantum mechanical superposition can sustain in the process of ion transfer in protein membranes for a substantial period despite interactions with environmental modes of molecular vibration. The ratio of decoherence time to dwell time can be greater than unity depending on mass, energy, and length parameters.
Source: arXiv:1303.1905
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/1303.1905
Date: 2013
Excerpt: "We showed that quantum mechanical superposition can sustain in the process of ion transfer in protein membrane for a substantial period in spite of the presence of the interactions with environmental modes of molecular vibration. The spectral temperature, as defined in quantum thermodynamical framework plays a significant role in maintaining the coherence."
Context: This paper uses quantum thermodynamics to argue that coherence in ion channels is more robust than previously thought, and suggests building quantum information systems driven by entangled ionic states.
Confidence: Medium (theoretical)
```

### 2.5 Quantum Memory Circuit for Ion Channel Dynamics (Sun & Zhang, 2024)

```
Claim: Time-varying voltage across ion channels induces an effective magnetic flux causing quantum coherence in ion tunnelings, leading to sideband effects in ion channel current dynamics that manifest as memory effects in the I-V curve hysteresis.
Source: arXiv:2411.12362
URL: https://arxiv.org/html/2411.12362v1
Date: 2024
Excerpt: "The time-varying voltage induces an effective magnetic flux which causes quantum coherence in ion tunnelings and leads to sideband effects in the ion channel current dynamics. The sideband effects in the ionic current dynamics manifest a multi-crossing hysteresis in the I-V curve, which is the memory dynamics responding to the variation of the external voltage."
Context: This recent work connects ion channel dynamics to quantum memory effects, suggesting a functional role for quantum coherence in neuronal signaling.
Confidence: Medium (recent preprint)
```

### 2.6 Mimicking Classical Noise in Ion Channels by Quantum Decoherence (Seifi et al., 2024)

```
Claim: Quantum decoherence can mimic classical noise in ion channels, suggesting that the distinction between classical and quantum descriptions of ion channel dynamics may be experimentally probed through decoherence measurements.
Source: Scientific Reports (Nature)
URL: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-67106-6
Date: 2024
Excerpt: "Mimicking classical noise in ion channels by quantum decoherence."
Context: This work bridges the quantum-classical divide in ion channel modeling, suggesting ways to experimentally test quantum effects.
Confidence: Medium
```

## 3. Synthetic and Artificial Systems

### 3.1 ENAQT in Perovskite Nanocrystal Superlattices -- Experimental (Wang et al., 2025)

```
Claim: Direct experimental visualization of coherent exciton transport and realization of ENAQT in CsPbBr3 nanocrystal superlattices. At intermediate temperatures, the interplay between coherence and dephasing collaboratively enhances transport, with a maximum steady-state diffusion constant at a turnover temperature (~70-100 K depending on sample).
Source: Nature Communications
URL: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-024-55812-8
Date: 2025
Excerpt: "Most remarkably, at intermediate temperatures, the interplay between coherence and dephasing collaboratively enhances transport. We observed a maximum for the steady-state diffusion constant at temperatures where static disorder and thermal fluctuations balance each other, providing experimental evidence of ENAQT."
Context: This is a landmark experimental paper -- the first clear experimental demonstration of ENAQT outside biological photosynthetic systems. The authors used transient absorption and photoluminescence microscopy over 7-298 K. The perovskite NC superlattices offer a highly controllable platform for investigating quantum transport.
Confidence: Very High (peer-reviewed experimental demonstration in Nature Communications)
```

### 3.2 Studying Light-Harvesting Models with Superconducting Circuits (Potocnik et al., 2018)

```
Claim: Superconducting circuit-based quantum simulator experimentally demonstrates environment-assisted quantum transport with both white noise and Lorentzian (structured) environmental noise. Transport efficiency shows a maximum at intermediate dephasing rates (characteristic ENAQT bell curve), with maximum efficiency of ~39%.
Source: Nature Communications
URL: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-018-03312-x
Date: 2018
Excerpt: "The transport efficiency shows a rapid increase from zero, a broad maximum of ~39%, and then a slow decrease with increasing pure dephasing rate, which are the characteristic features of noise-assisted transport. The decrease in efficiency above an optimal noise power is due to dephasing-induced population localization, also referred to as the quantum Zeno effect."
Context: This is the first superconducting circuit experimental simulation of ENAQT. The system used four qubits with tailored energies and couplings to match the FMO complex structure. The maximum efficiency occurs at the strong-to-weak coupling crossover.
Confidence: Very High (experimental demonstration in Nature Communications)
```

### 3.3 Noise-Assisted Transport in All-Optical Cavity-Based Network (Viciani et al., 2015)

```
Claim: First experimental demonstration of noise-assisted transport in an all-optical cavity-based network. Transport efficiency reaches a maximum when varying external dephasing noise, showing the characteristic bell-like shape predicted theoretically for ENAQT.
Source: Physical Review Letters
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/1504.04809
Date: 2015
Excerpt: "We realize simple, scalable, and controllable optical fiber cavity networks that allow us to analyze the performance of transport networks for different conditions of interference, dephasing and disorder. In particular, we experimentally demonstrate that the transport efficiency reaches a maximum when varying the external dephasing noise, i.e., a bell-like shape behavior that had been predicted only theoretically."
Context: This was the first experimental demonstration of ENAQT in a purely photonic system, using optical fiber cavity networks. The system is scalable and controllable, making it a promising simulator of quantum transport phenomena.
Confidence: Very High (PRL experimental demonstration)
```

### 3.4 Enhancing Coherent Transport in Photonic Networks (Biggerstaff et al., 2016)

```
Claim: Experimental simulation of environment-assisted coherent transport using engineered networks of laser-written waveguides. Controllable decoherence simulated by broadening illumination bandwidth yields significant increase in transport efficiency.
Source: Nature Communications
URL: https://www.nature.com/articles/ncomms11282
Date: 2016
Excerpt: "We report an experimental simulation of environment-assisted coherent transport, using an engineered network of laser-written waveguides, with relative energies and inter-waveguide couplings tailored to yield the desired Hamiltonian. Controllable-strength decoherence is simulated by broadening the bandwidth of the input illumination, yielding a significant increase in transport efficiency relative to the narrowband case."
Context: This work demonstrated integrated optics as suitable for simulating specific target Hamiltonians as well as open quantum systems with controllable loss and decoherence.
Confidence: Very High (Nature Communications experimental)
```

### 3.5 From Goldilocks to Twin Peaks: Multiple Optimal ENAQT Regimes (Coates et al., 2023)

```
Claim: A consistent subset (~6%) of physically modelled transport networks can have at least two ENAQT peaks in their steady-state transport efficiency, challenging the assumption of a single optimal environmental coupling rate. Systems with large gaps in eigenenergy distributions are most likely to exhibit this behavior.
Source: Physical Chemistry Chemical Physics
URL: https://pubs.rsc.org/en/content/articlelanding/2023/cp/d2cp04935j
Date: 2023
Excerpt: "In this paper we show that a consistent subset of physically modelled transport networks can have at least two ENAQT peaks in their steady state transport efficiency... We consistently find about 6% of networks have multiple peaks in their transport efficiency, regardless of the spectral density or temperature."
Context: This paper challenges the conventional "Goldilocks" picture of ENAQT (one optimal noise level) and reveals more complex transport landscapes in disordered networks, relevant to both natural and artificial systems.
Confidence: High (peer-reviewed computational study)
```

## 4. Organic Molecules and Receptor-Ligand Complexes

### 4.1 ENAQT in Organic Molecules -- Caffeine (Vattay et al., 2015)

```
Claim: ENAQT makes cross-ligand electron transport possible and efficient between certain atoms in organic molecules (demonstrated on caffeine at room temperature). This effect can potentially change understanding of how receptors work by enabling charge reorganization when ligand molecules dock.
Source: arXiv:1503.00178
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/1503.00178
Date: 2015
Excerpt: "One of the new discoveries in quantum biology is the role of Environment Assisted Quantum Transport (ENAQT) in excitonic transport processes... This poised realm between the pure quantum and the semi-classical domains has not been considered in other biological transport processes, such as charge transport through organic molecules. Binding in receptor-ligand complexes is assumed to be static as electrons are assumed to be not able to cross the ligand molecule. We show that ENAQT makes cross ligand transport possible and efficient between certain atoms opening the way for the reorganization of the charge distribution on the receptor when the ligand molecule docks. We demonstrate room temperature ENAQT on the caffeine molecule."
Context: This is a significant paper as it extends ENAQT from excitonic energy transfer to charge transport in organic molecules, suggesting a potential role in receptor function and drug binding.
Confidence: Medium (arXiv preprint; conceptually important but not experimentally verified in biological context)
```

## 5. DNA Charge Transport

### 5.1 Hole Transfer Through DNA -- Two Regimes (Giese et al., 2001)

```
Claim: Experimental observation of two distinct charge transfer regimes in DNA: coherent superexchange (tunneling) at short distances (up to 3 base pairs) and thermally induced hopping between adenine bases at longer distances. The transition between regimes occurs at ~3-4 bridging base pairs.
Source: Nature
URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11460159/
Date: 2001
Excerpt: "We show experimentally that the rate of charge transfer between two guanine bases decreases with increasing separation only if the guanines are separated by no more than three base pairs; if more bridging base pairs are present, the transfer rates exhibit only a weak distance dependence. We attribute this distinct change in the distance dependence of the rate of charge transfer through DNA to a shift from coherent superexchange charge transfer (tunnelling) at short distances to a process mediated by thermally induced hopping of charges between adenine bases (A-hopping) at long distances."
Context: This landmark study established the dual-mechanism nature of DNA charge transport. The thermal hopping regime involves sequential charge transfer steps that may benefit from environmental interactions similar to ENAQT, though ENAQT proper has not been directly demonstrated in DNA.
Confidence: High (Nature experimental paper)
```

### 5.2 Quantum Charge Transport in DNA/RNA with Nanoribbon Electrodes

```
Claim: Theoretical study of quantum charge transport through DNA and RNA nanowire models using tight-binding Hamiltonian and Landauer-Buttiker formalism. Dimerization effects of hopping parameters can make DNA/RNA operate as semiconductor or conductor.
Source: PMC
URL: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12864597/
Date: 2025
Excerpt: "In the domain of biological nanosystems like DNA, the typical theoretical model for electron transport... uses perfect leads... electron-phonon interactions convert DNA/RNA from basic conductors into intricate quantum systems, wherein vibrations play an active role in charge transport. This transformation results in phenomena such as energy dissipation, state renormalization, and modified coherence."
Context: Computational study of DNA as a quantum nanowire, showing how vibrational coupling (environmental effects) modify charge transport. The connection to ENAQT is indirect -- the phonon-assisted transport shows environmental influence but not necessarily the specific ENAQT enhancement mechanism.
Confidence: Medium (computational study)
```

### 5.3 Assessment: Is ENAQT Active in DNA Charge Transport?

**Verdict: Not directly demonstrated.** While DNA charge transport exhibits quantum effects (coherent tunneling at short distances, thermal hopping at long distances), and phonon-assisted transport has been studied, the specific ENAQT mechanism -- where an optimal level of environmental dephasing maximizes transport efficiency -- has not been explicitly demonstrated or proposed for DNA. The closest connection is the phonon-assisted hopping mechanism at long distances, which is conceptually related but distinct from ENAQT.

## 6. Olfaction and Quantum Vibration Theory

### 6.1 Quantum Vibration Theory of Olfaction (Turin, 1996; Various Follow-ups)

```
Claim: The vibrational theory of olfaction proposes that odorant detection involves inelastic electron tunneling through odorant molecules, with molecular vibrations providing the energy matching condition. Recent experimental evidence shows fruit flies can distinguish odorants with identical shapes but different isotopes.
Source: Various (Nature 2001 for isotope discrimination; multiple theoretical papers)
URL: https://johnjoemcfadden.co.uk/popular-science/quantum-biology/smell/
Date: Ongoing research since 1996
Excerpt: "Turin proposed that when odour molecules are captured by olfactory receptors their bonds vibrations promote a quantum tunnelling event in the receptor molecule that, eventually, sends a signal to the brain. The theory received a boost when it was recently found that fruit flies can distinguish odorants with exactly the same shape but made of different isotopes of the same elements, something that is hard to explain without quantum mechanics."
Context: While the vibrational theory involves quantum tunneling and environmental coupling (the receptor provides the thermal environment), the specific ENAQT mechanism -- dephasing-assisted enhancement of transport efficiency -- has not been directly proposed or demonstrated for olfaction. The theory focuses on electron tunneling through odorants, not transport efficiency optimization.
Confidence: Low-Medium for ENAQT specifically (the olfactory tunneling theory is debated but not directly connected to ENAQT)
```

### 6.2 Assessment: Is ENAQT Relevant to Olfaction?

**Verdict: Not directly.** While olfaction may involve quantum tunneling, the ENAQT mechanism (optimal dephasing enhancing transport) is not the operative concept. Olfactory detection is about signal transduction (does an electron tunnel?), not about optimizing transport efficiency across a network. The thermal environment plays a role (as required by Marcus theory of electron transfer), but this is different from the ENAQT optimization mechanism.

## 7. Mitochondrial Electron Transport

### 7.1 Proton Pumping in Complex I -- Quantum Dot Analogy

```
Claim: Electron transport in mitochondrial complexes occurs as hopping between metal atoms (FeS clusters) embedded into proteins, analogous to hopping between semiconductor quantum dots. The model treats biological systems as "natural quantum dots" with energy levels well-separated enough to remain discrete at physiological temperature.
Source: Quantum Reports (MDPI)
URL: https://www.mdpi.com/2624-960X/3/3/27
Date: 2021
Excerpt: "Within the mitochondrial complexes, electron transport occurs as hopping between metal atoms (or FeS clusters) embedded into the proteins, similar to the hopping between semiconductor quantum dots. Such 0D nanostructures are frequently called 'artificial atoms', and we extend the similarity by calling atoms 'natural quantum dots'... In biological systems, energy levels are so well separated that they remain discrete even at elevated temperatures."
Context: This paper uses a quantum Heisenberg equation approach to model Complex I, but does not explicitly demonstrate or propose ENAQT. The electron-proton energy exchange is modeled via conformational changes (piston model) rather than dephasing-assisted transport.
Confidence: Low for ENAQT specifically (quantum modeling exists, but ENAQT mechanism not identified)
```

### 7.2 Assessment: Is ENAQT Active in Mitochondrial Electron Transport?

**Verdict: No direct evidence or proposal found.** While mitochondrial electron transport is increasingly recognized as involving quantum effects (tunneling between FeS clusters, quantum dot-like behavior), the specific ENAQT mechanism has not been proposed or demonstrated for Complex I or any other mitochondrial complex. The coupling between electron transfer and conformational changes (electrostatic wave/piston model) represents a different class of quantum-classical interaction.

## 8. Ordered Systems and New ENAQT Mechanisms

### 8.1 ENAQT in Ordered Systems (Kassal & Aspuru-Guzik, 2012/2018)

```
Claim: Several new mechanisms of environment-assisted transport exist in ordered systems where there is no localization to overcome and where coherent transport would naively be expected to be fastest. The model (tight-binding with dephasing, source and trap) has wider application beyond photosynthesis.
Source: New Journal of Physics / Harvard DASH
URL: https://dash.harvard.edu/entities/publication/73120378-c166-6bd4-e053-0100007fdf3b
Date: 2012/2018
Excerpt: "We report several new mechanisms of environment-assisted transport in ordered systems, in which there is no localization to overcome and where one would naively expect that coherent transport is the fastest possible. Although we are particularly motivated by the need to understand excitonic energy transfer in photosynthetic light-harvesting complexes, our model is general -- transport in a tight-binding system with dephasing, a source and a trap -- and can be expected to have wider application."
Context: This work significantly broadens the scope of ENAQT by showing it can occur even in ordered systems without Anderson localization. The mechanisms are different from the standard disorder-delocalization picture and may apply to a wider range of biological and synthetic systems.
Confidence: High (peer-reviewed theoretical work)
```

### 8.2 Universal Origin for ENAQT (Zerah-Harush & Dubi, 2018)

```
Claim: ENAQT has a universal origin based on two competing processes: (1) dephasing tendency to make exciton population uniform, and (2) formation of an exciton density gradient defined by source and sink. A geometric condition exists: ENAQT only appears in non-symmetric networks (those lacking inversion symmetry).
Source: Journal of Physical Chemistry Letters
URL: https://pubs.acs.org/doi/10.1021/acs.jpclett.7b03306
Date: 2018
Excerpt: "We show that ENAQT appears due to two competing processes, namely, the tendency of dephasing to make the exciton population uniform, and the formation of an exciton density gradient, defined by the source and the sink. Furthermore, we find a geometric condition on the network for the appearance of ENAQT, relevant to natural and artificial systems."
Context: This paper provides a universal framework for understanding when ENAQT can occur. The geometric condition (non-symmetry requirement) is a powerful predictive tool for identifying candidate systems.
Confidence: High (peer-reviewed, widely cited)
```

## 9. Testable Predictions of ENAQT

Based on the literature review, the key testable predictions that ENAQT makes for any system are:

### 9.1 The ENAQT Bell Curve (Primary Signature)

```
Prediction: Transport efficiency as a function of dephasing rate (or temperature) should show a non-monotonic behavior -- increasing from zero at low dephasing, reaching a maximum at an intermediate dephasing rate, and then decreasing at high dephasing (quantum Zeno regime).
Source: Rebentrost et al. 2009; confirmed in multiple experimental systems
Relevance: This is the defining signature of ENAQT. The turnover temperature/dephasing rate marks the balance between static disorder and thermal fluctuations.
Status: Experimentally confirmed in perovskite NC superlattices (2025), superconducting circuits (2018), optical cavity networks (2015), and photonic networks (2016).
```

### 9.2 Turnover Temperature

```
Prediction: For a given system with static disorder strength Delta, the optimal transport occurs when the dephasing rate Gamma approximately equals Delta (Gamma ~ Delta). This predicts a turnover temperature T_t where transport is maximized.
Source: Wang et al. 2025 (Nature Communications)
Relevance: Enables quantitative prediction of the optimal operating temperature for any candidate system.
Status: Confirmed experimentally in perovskite NC superlattices.
```

### 9.3 Non-Symmetry Requirement

```
Prediction: ENAQT requires broken inversion symmetry in the transport network. Symmetric systems with source and sink at opposite ends will not exhibit ENAQT.
Source: Zerah-Harush & Dubi 2018
Relevance: Provides a structural criterion for identifying candidate systems. Ion channels (asymmetric selectivity filters) and microtubules (helical structure) may satisfy this.
Status: Theoretically derived; needs experimental validation in biological systems.
```

### 9.4 Multiple Optimal Regimes

```
Prediction: In some disordered network geometries, transport efficiency can have TWO or more peaks as a function of dephasing rate, not just one.
Source: Coates et al. 2023
Relevance: Complex transport networks (like microtubule tryptophan networks) may exhibit multiple ENAQT optima.
Status: Computationally demonstrated in ~6% of random dipole networks.
```

### 9.5 Convergence of Transient and Steady-State Transport

```
Prediction: At the ENAQT turnover point, the transient diffusion constant D_max should converge with the steady-state diffusion constant D_s.
Source: Wang et al. 2025
Relevance: This convergence provides an independent signature of ENAQT that can be measured in time-resolved experiments.
Status: Experimentally observed in perovskite NC superlattices.
```

### 9.6 Temperature-Dependent Anesthetic Effects

```
Prediction: If microtubule energy transport operates via ENAQT, anesthetics that alter the dephasing environment should shift the optimal transport temperature and reduce the maximum diffusion length.
Source: Kalra et al. 2023 (demonstrated etomidate and isoflurane reduce exciton diffusion)
Relevance: Provides a direct pharmacological test of ENAQT in microtubules.
Status: Anesthetics shown to reduce diffusion, but the full ENAQT curve with anesthetics has not been mapped.
```

### Key Conclusions

1. **Experimental ENAQT outside photosynthesis HAS been achieved**, but only in synthetic/artificial systems (perovskite NC superlattices, superconducting circuits, photonic networks, optical cavities). No direct experimental demonstration exists in a non-photosynthetic biological system.

2. **Microtubule tryptophan networks are the most promising biological candidate** for ENAQT beyond photosynthesis. Long-range energy migration has been experimentally demonstrated (Kalra 2023), and computational studies show coherence (Craddock 2014) and ENAQT-like behavior (Gassab 2026). However, the specific ENAQT bell curve has not been experimentally mapped.

3. **Ion channel selectivity filters have a strong computational case** for ENAQT (Bassereh 2015; Jalalinejad 2018), but experimental verification is lacking.

4. **The universal geometric condition** (Zerah-Harush & Dubi 2018) provides a powerful tool for identifying new candidate systems: any non-symmetric transport network with static disorder and environmental dephasing is a potential ENAQT system.

5. **Multiple optimal regimes** (Coates 2023) suggest that complex biological networks may have richer transport landscapes than previously appreciated.

6. **The ordered-system mechanisms** (Kassal & Aspuru-Guzik 2012/2018) broaden ENAQT's applicability beyond disordered systems, potentially relevant to the crystalline order of microtubules.

### Research Gaps and Future Directions

- Direct experimental measurement of the ENAQT bell curve in microtubule tryptophan networks (temperature-dependent energy transport)
- Experimental verification of ENAQT in ion channel selectivity filters
- Connection between ENAQT and anesthetic action on microtubules
- Role of ENAQT in DNA damage recognition and repair
- Quantum simulation of ENAQT in increasingly complex biological network geometries
